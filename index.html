Station 01 Primary Quote
"
Where they burn books, they will ultimately burn people also.
— Heinrich Heine
German-Jewish poet, 1820 — from his play Almansor
Historical Context
Heine wrote this in 1820, referring to the burning of the Quran during the Spanish Inquisition. More than 100 years later, the Nazis burned his books in the same square where he had written these words. Heine was Jewish. Six million Jews were later murdered in the Holocaust.

What You're Looking At
On the night of May 10, 1933, members of the Nazi Student Union and the Hitler Youth gathered in Berlin's Bebelplatz square and burned more than 25,000 books. The books targeted included works by Jewish authors, political opponents, and anyone the Nazi regime considered "un-German." Joseph Goebbels, Nazi Minister of Propaganda, gave a speech at the bonfire. The burnings occurred in 34 university towns across Germany that same night.
"These flames do not only illuminate the final end of an old era; they also light up the new."
— Joseph Goebbels, May 10, 1933

Station 03 Author's Words
"
Television gives you the dates of Napoleon but not who he was as a human. It gives you William Shakespeare, but it doesn't follow you through the night and let you walk the fields with him, it doesn't let you smell thosefields, or touch that network of friends, or talk with him about his ideas.
— Ray Bradbury
From an interview, 2010 — Bradbury refused to convert F451 to an e-book until shortly before his death
Think About It
Bradbury wrote Fahrenheit 451 partly as a reaction to the rise of television in the 1950s. He believed TV was creating a culture that preferred speed and images over the slow, deep engagement required by books. In the novel, Mildred spends her days watching the "parlor walls" — massive interactive screens. Bradbury called it "social happiness."

Station 05 Primary Quote
"
Once you learn to read, you will be forever free.
"Knowledge makes a man unfit to be a slave."
— Frederick Douglass
Escaped enslaved person, abolitionist, and orator — 1817–1895
Historical Context
In antebellum America, it was illegal to teach enslaved people to read. Slaveholders understood that literacy meant access to ideas, to legal arguments, to the ability to communicate across distances, and to the knowledge that one's condition was not inevitable. Douglass secretly taught himself to read as a child after his enslaver forbade it. He later called that moment — understanding why literacy was suppressed — the turning point of his life. His autobiography became one of the most powerful antislavery texts ever written.

What You're Looking At
In 2001, the Taliban destroyed much of Afghanistan's cultural heritage, including thousands of books and manuscripts. In 2021, after returning to power, the Taliban immediately banned girls from secondary school and university education, and burned books considered "un-Islamic." In 2015, ISIS burned an estimated 8,000 books from the Mosul Public Library in Iraq, throwing manuscripts dating back to the 7th century into the streets. The pattern: destroy memory, rewrite culture.

Books These Authors Wrote That Have Been Banned
Margaret Atwood's The Handmaid's Tale — banned for sexual content and "anti-Christian" themes. Toni Morrison's Beloved — challenged for "violent content" and "controversial" subject matter (American slavery). Both authors have publicly advocated against censorship.
